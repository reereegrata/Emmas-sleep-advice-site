White Noise Safety — What Red Nose Australia Says About Decibel Levels

White noise is safe — when you use it right. The key risk is volume. A 2014 study in the journal Pediatrics tested 14 infant sleep machines and found that all of them could produce sound levels above 50 decibels when placed close to the cot. At maximum volume and close range, three machines exceeded 85 dB — the equivalent of heavy city traffic.

Red Nose Australia and the American Academy of Pediatrics both recommend:

  • Keep it at 50 decibels or below — about the volume of a quiet conversation or a shower running in the next room. Most machines have a volume dial — keep it at the halfway point or lower.
  • Place it at least 2 metres from the cot — never on the cot rail, headboard, or mattress. Sound intensity drops by half with every doubling of distance.
  • Never inside or attached to the cot — crib-mounted machines concentrate sound in a small space. The cot is for your baby and a fitted sheet, nothing else.
  • Use a timer — there's no evidence that white noise needs to play all night to be effective. A 30–60 minute timer that covers the falling-asleep window is perfectly fine.
  • Test with a free decibel app — download a decibel meter app (Decibel X or NIOSH SLM are both free), place your phone where your baby's head would be, and check the reading.

Quick test: Stand next to the cot with the machine running at its usual spot. If you can hold a normal conversation without raising your voice, the volume is likely safe. If you have to speak up to be heard, turn it down.

Frequently Asked Questions

How loud should a baby white noise machine be?+
Red Nose Australia and the AAP recommend 50 decibels or below at the cot — about the volume of a quiet conversation or a running shower from a closed bathroom. Place the machine at least 2 metres from your baby's cot and never attach it to the rail. Download a free decibel meter app (Decibel X or NIOSH SLM) to check.
What is the best white noise machine for babies in Australia?+
For most Australian families, the Hatch Rest 2 (AU$109-129) is our top pick — app-controlled, 18 sounds, doubles as a night light and toddler OK-to-wake clock. For budget, go with the Welcare Sleep-Tight (AU$59). For portability, the Dreamegg D1 (AU$69) wins.
Does white noise actually help babies sleep?+
Yes. White noise mimics the loud, constant whooshing sound babies heard 24/7 in the womb. A 2020 study in Archives of Disease in Childhood found that 80% of newborns fell asleep within 5 minutes with white noise vs only 25% without. It also masks sudden household noises — doorbells, dogs barking, older siblings — that might otherwise wake a light-sleeping baby.
Can white noise damage my baby's hearing?+
Not when used correctly. The risk comes from volume and proximity — any machine cranked to maximum and placed right next to a baby's head can be too loud. Follow the 50 dB / 2-metre rule: keep volume at or below 50 decibels and place the machine at least 2 metres from the cot. Red Nose Australia confirms that white noise used at safe volumes poses no hearing risk to infants.
Portable vs nursery white noise machine — which do I need?+
A portable machine (Dreamegg D1, ergoPouch Drift Away, Baby Shusher) is best if you travel regularly, use it in the pram or car, or move between rooms. A nursery machine (Hatch Rest 2, Glow Dreaming) is better as a permanent bedroom setup with extra features like night lights and toddler clocks. Many families buy one of each — a Hatch or Glow Dreaming for the nursery + a Dreamegg D1 or Baby Shusher for on-the-go.
