Room Temperature for Baby Sleep, How to Dress Your Baby for Australian Weather

By Emma · July 2026 · 7 min read
Home › Guides › Room Temperature Guide
← See our top sleep sack picks

The safest room temperature for a baby to sleep in is 18–22°C, according to Red Nose Australia. and overheating is a known risk factor for SIDS. This is why room temperature matters as much as safe sleep position. Australian homes, with their wide temperature swings from tropical summers to freezing winter nights, make getting the room temperature right both important and challenging.

This guide covers how to check your baby's temperature, which TOG rating to choose by season, and exactly how to dress your baby for sleep at every age and temperature. We also cover heaters, fans, room thermometers, and the signs your baby is too hot or too cold.

What Is the Safest Room Temperature for a Baby?

Red Nose Australia recommends 18–22°C as the safe room temperature range for baby sleep. This range balances warmth with the reduced SIDS risk associated with cooler temperatures. Babies cannot regulate their body temperature as well as adults, they overheat faster and lose heat faster.

The rule of thumb: If you're comfortable in light clothing, your baby probably is too. If you feel cold, your baby likely needs an extra layer. Always use a room thermometer. not guesswork; to know the actual temperature.

The risks at each extreme are different. Below 16°C, a baby can get too cold, which increases the risk of respiratory infections. Above 24°C, overheating becomes a serious concern, and overheating is a known SIDS risk factor. Most Australian homes without climate control swing between these extremes across seasons, which is why dressing your baby appropriately matters.

How to Check If Your Baby Is Too Hot or Too Cold

The back of the neck (or the tummy) is the most reliable place to check your baby's temperature. Hands and feet are naturally cooler and are not a reliable indicator.

Signs your baby is too hot:

  • Back of the neck feels sweaty or damp
  • Flushed or red cheeks
  • Rapid breathing
  • Hair is damp with sweat
  • Baby seems restless or irritable

Signs your baby is too cold:

  • Back of the neck feels cool to the touch
  • Tummy feels cool
  • Baby is unusually still or quiet
  • Pale or mottled skin

If your baby is too hot, remove a layer and wait 5–10 minutes before checking again. If too cold, add a layer (a singlet under the sleeping bag, or a warmer TOG). Red Nose advises against using a hat for sleep — babies release excess heat through their head.

How to Dress Your Baby for Sleep (by Age)

0–3 months (Newborn)

Newborns can't regulate their temperature well. In a room at 20–22°C, dress your newborn in a singlet, a long-sleeve bodysuit, and a swaddle or 1.0 TOG sleeping bag. Check their neck every time you check on them. Newborns should never wear a hat to sleep.

3–6 months (Rolling stage)

By this age, your baby has likely transitioned from a swaddle to a sleeping bag with arms free. For 20–22°C, a 1.0 TOG bag over a long-sleeve bodysuit works for most babies. For colder rooms, layer a singlet underneath and move up to 2.5 TOG. Watch for the first signs of rolling and stop swaddling immediately when you see them.

6–12 months (Older baby)

Your baby is more active and moving around the cot. A 2.5 TOG sleeping bag is the workhorse for most Australian babies through autumn, winter, and spring. Dress in a bodysuit plus lightweight pyjamas underneath. In summer, a 0.5 TOG bag with a short-sleeve bodysuit is usually enough.

Winter Baby Sleep — Dressing for Cold Australian Nights

Australian winter nights can drop well below the recommended 18°C minimum in many homes, particularly older houses without central heating. Dressing your baby properly becomes essential.

Winter dressing strategy (room 14–18°C):

  • Layer 1: Long-sleeve bodysuit
  • Layer 2: Warm pyjamas (cotton or fleece)
  • Layer 3: 2.5 or 3.5 TOG sleeping bag

If using a heater, set the thermostat to 18–20°C and place it at least one metre from the cot. Oil column heaters are the safest option for nurseries — they don't have exposed elements and maintain a steady temperature. Never use a portable gas heater in a baby's room; they produce carbon monoxide.

Red Nose winter safety: Do not use hot water bottles, electric blankets, or wheat bags in your baby's cot. They can overheat a baby rapidly and are not recommended under Red Nose safe sleep guidelines.

Summer Baby Sleep — Keeping Cool Without a Blanket

Overheating is a bigger risk than being slightly cool, particularly in Australian summer. When the temperature climbs above 24°C in the nursery, take these steps:

  • Dress your baby in a nappy and a 0.2 TOG sleeping bag (or no bag at all if the room is over 26°C)
  • Use a fan to circulate air — point it away from the cot, not directly at the baby
  • Keep blinds or curtains closed during the day to stop the room heating up
  • If using air conditioning, set it to 22–24°C — not too cold
  • Offer extra feeds on hot nights — breastmilk or formula helps with hydration

A lukewarm bath before bed can help lower your baby's body temperature on a hot evening. Avoid cold baths, which can shock a baby's system.

Room Thermometers — Do You Really Need One?

A room thermometer is a small investment that takes the guesswork out of dressing your baby. Many parents rely on how the room feels to them, but what feels warm to an adult in pyjamas can feel very different to a baby in a sleeping bag. A basic digital room thermometer costs around AU$10–15 and gives you an objective reading every time.

Some baby monitors include a room temperature sensor, which is convenient. If you're using one, place the sensor near the cot at mattress level — not near a window or heater where the reading will be inaccurate.

Blackout Curtains and Blinds for Your Baby's Room

If your baby wakes at 5 am with the sun or fights bedtime because it is still bright outside at 8 pm, blackout curtains might be the single most impactful change you can make. In Australia, summer daylight stretches well past 8 pm in most states, and the early morning sun in spring and summer can hit the nursery window before 6 am. A dark room signals to your baby's brain that it is sleep time, which supports their natural melatonin production and circadian rhythm.

There are three main options for Australian homes. Blockout curtains are the most common and cost roughly AU$40 to $150 per panel depending on size and brand. They hang from a curtain rod and work best when the rod extends past the window frame so light does not leak in at the sides. Blackout blinds, which fit inside the window recess, cost roughly AU$30 to $80 and are more space-efficient for smaller nursery windows. Window film is the cheapest option — around AU$15 to $30 — and sticks directly to the glass, though it is less effective than curtains or blinds at blocking the brightest direct sun.

Installation matters just as much as the product itself. Even a narrow gap at the top or sides of a curtain will let in a surprising amount of light. Use a curtain pelmet, overlap two curtain panels, or add side channels if light leakage is a problem. For blinds, an inside-mount fit reduces gaps but an outside mount that covers the entire window frame often blocks more light.

Safety is non-negotiable. All blinds and curtains sold in Australia must comply with mandatory cord safety standards (AS/NZS 4215). Choose cordless options wherever possible. If your existing blinds have cords, they must be secured with a cleat or tension device and kept well out of reach of the cot — at least one metre away. Strangulation from blind and curtain cords is a real risk once your baby becomes mobile.

Blackout curtains help most with early morning waking, summer bedtime resistance, and the daylight saving transitions in October and April when the clock shifts but the sun does not. Pair them with a consistent bedtime routine, and consider a white noise machine to complement the dark environment with steady background sound.

Light and Noise in Your Baby's Sleep Environment

Temperature, light, and noise are the three pillars of a baby's sleep environment. You have already covered temperature in this guide. Light and noise are the other two pieces that turn a bedroom into a sleep sanctuary — or keep your baby fighting sleep at every nap and bedtime.

Light is the strongest signal your baby's brain uses to know whether it is time to be awake or asleep. In the first three months, your newborn's circadian rhythm is still developing and light has less impact on sleep patterns. This is why newborns sleep around the clock regardless of whether the room is bright or dark. After three to four months, the rhythm matures and light becomes much more influential. A dark room — truly dark, not just dim — helps trigger melatonin release and keeps your baby sleeping through those brief surfacing moments between sleep cycles.

What about night lights? If you need light for night feeds or nappy changes, use a red or amber night light, not white or blue. Red and amber wavelengths do not suppress melatonin production, so they are less likely to wake your baby fully or make it harder for you to fall back asleep. Avoid screen light — your phone, a tablet, or the baby monitor screen — in the nursery at night. Blue light from screens is the most disruptive to melatonin.

Noise is the second pillar. Babies do not need silence to sleep. In fact, complete silence can make sleep harder because every small household noise — a floorboard creaking, a door closing, a dog barking — becomes a startling disruption. Consistent background sound creates an acoustic blanket that smooths over these random noises. White noise is the most studied option, but pink noise and brown noise have deeper frequencies that some babies prefer. All are safe as long as the volume stays at or below 50 decibels measured at cot distance, which is about the volume of a quiet conversation or gentle rainfall. See our comparison of white, pink, and brown noise for help choosing, and our evidence-based guide on whether white noise helps baby sleep explains the research behind it.

The ideal baby sleep environment: 18 to 22 degrees Celsius, pitch dark, and steady background sound at or below 50 decibels measured at cot distance. Get these three right, and you have removed the environmental barriers to good sleep. The rest is developmental and will come with time.

Australian Seasons and Room Temperature Challenges

Australia's climate creates unique room temperature challenges that a general guide cannot fully cover. A January night in Darwin feels nothing like a July night in Hobart, and homes across the country vary enormously in how well they handle temperature extremes.

Regional differences matter. In tropical areas like Darwin, Cairns, and northern Queensland, humidity is the bigger challenge. High humidity makes a room feel hotter than the thermometer reads, and babies can overheat faster in muggy conditions. Evaporative coolers work well in dry heat but struggle in humidity. In humid climates, a split-system air conditioner is more effective. In southern cities like Melbourne, Adelaide, and Hobart, winter nights regularly drop well below the recommended 18-degree minimum, and older homes without central heating need a strategy. Portable oil column heaters with thermostats are the safest option — they maintain a steady temperature without exposed elements — but must be placed at least one metre from the cot and never left running all night without a thermostat.

A quick seasonal checklist for Australian parents:

  • Summer (December to February): Blockout curtains closed during the day, a fan circulating air (not pointed at the cot), a 0.2 or 0.5 TOG sleep sack or just a nappy on the hottest nights, and a room thermometer to track whether the temperature drops enough overnight to need an extra layer.
  • Winter (June to August): Oil column heater with thermostat set to 18 to 20 degrees, a 2.5 to 3.5 TOG sleep sack, layered clothing underneath (singlet plus warm pyjamas), and check the back of your baby's neck at each night feed to confirm they are warm but not sweaty.
  • Spring and autumn (September to November, March to May): The tricky in-between months when daytime and overnight temperatures can swing 10 to 15 degrees. Dress for the overnight low, not the daytime high. A 1.0 TOG bag with a singlet and long-sleeve bodysuit covers most spring and autumn nights. Keep the room thermometer visible and check it before bed. If the forecast says a cold change is coming through at 3 am, add a layer at bedtime rather than trying to add it later.

This section focuses specifically on how Australian seasons affect room temperature management. Common Questions About Room Temperature

What temperature should a baby's room be in winter?

Red Nose Australia recommends keeping your baby's room between 18–22°C year-round. In winter, this often means using a heater with a thermostat. Avoid overheating — a room above 22°C increases SIDS risk. Use a room thermometer to check, not guess.

How do I know if my baby is too cold at night?

Check the back of your baby's neck or their tummy — not their hands or feet, which are naturally cooler. A cold neck or tummy means they need another layer. A sweaty or hot neck means they're overdressed. Red Nose advises that babies should feel comfortably warm, not hot to the touch.

Can a baby sleep in just a nappy in summer?

Yes, if the room temperature exceeds 25°C. In an Australian summer heatwave with no air conditioning, a nappy and a 0.2 TOG sleeping bag (or no bag) are appropriate. Watch for signs of overheating — flushed cheeks, rapid breathing, damp hair. A fan circulating air (not blowing directly on the baby) helps.

What TOG sleeping bag for 22 degrees?

For a room temperature of 22°C, use a 1.0 TOG sleeping bag with a short-sleeve bodysuit underneath. If your baby tends to feel warm, a 0.5 TOG bag is also suitable. Always check your baby's neck to confirm they're comfortable — TOG guides are starting points, not rules.

Should I use a heater in my baby's room overnight?

Only if the room regularly drops below 16°C. If using a heater, choose one with a thermostat set to 18–20°C, place it at least one metre from the cot, and never use it to heat the cot directly. Oil column heaters are generally the safest option for nurseries. Never use portable gas heaters — they produce carbon monoxide.
