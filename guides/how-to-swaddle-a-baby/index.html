How to Swaddle a Baby in Australia — Safe Step-by-Step Guide

By Emma · June 2026 · 5 min read
← See our top swaddle picks

Swaddling is one of the most effective ways to help your newborn feel safe and sleep longer. It calms their startle reflex, mimics the snugness of the womb, and can add precious hours to your sleep. But doing it wrong is dangerous — which is why Red Nose Australia has clear, evidence-based safety guidelines every Australian parent should follow.

This guide walks you through exactly how to swaddle your baby — step by step — using techniques approved by Red Nose Australia. Whether you're using a zip-up swaddle, a velcro wrap, or a traditional muslin blanket, you'll know exactly what to do before you leave the hospital.

What Is a Swaddle? (And Why It Helps Your Baby Sleep)

A swaddle is a wrap that keeps your baby's arms gently secured against their body, preventing the startle reflex from waking them. It can be a square muslin blanket, a zip-up sack, or a velcro wrap — the principle is the same: recreate the snug, secure feeling of the womb.

Newborns are born with a strong startle reflex — also called the Moro reflex. It's that sudden arm-jerk you see when they're drifting off to sleep. It wakes them up, and in the first few months it can fire 10-15 times a night. Every. Single. Night.

A properly fitted swaddle stops the startle reflex from waking your baby by keeping their arms gently contained. It recreates the snug, secure feeling of the womb — which is why most swaddled babies settle faster, cry less, and sleep for longer stretches. Australian hospitals teach swaddling as a standard part of newborn care, and research shows it reduces spontaneous waking by up to 30%.

Did you know? Red Nose Australia recommends swaddling from birth — but only when done safely. Always place your swaddled baby on their back. Stop swaddling the moment they show any signs of rolling.

How to Swaddle a Baby — Step-by-Step Instructions

Here's the classic technique using a square muslin or cotton wrap.

Step 1 — Lay the Swaddle Flat

Spread a square wrap on a flat surface in a diamond shape. Fold the top corner down about 15cm (6 inches) to create a straight edge. The fold line will sit across your baby's shoulders. If you're using a muslin, the diamond orientation gives you the longest wings to wrap with.

Step 2 — Position Your Baby

Place your baby on their back with their shoulders level with the folded edge. Their neck should be above the fold — never cover their face. Their arms should rest naturally at their sides or slightly bent.

Step 3 — Wrap the First Side

Take the left corner of the wrap and pull it across your baby's chest. Tuck it firmly under their right side. The wrap should be snug around the arm but loose around the hips — their legs need room to bend and move freely.

Step 4 — Secure the Swaddle

Fold the bottom corner up over your baby's feet — but not too tight. Then take the right corner and wrap it across their chest, tucking it under their left side. The final wrap should be firm around the arms but allow two fingers between the fabric and your baby's chest.

How to Swaddle Safely — The Red Nose Australia Checklist

Red Nose Australia is the country's leading authority on SIDS prevention. Here's their complete safe swaddling checklist:

  • Always on their back — never place a swaddled baby on their tummy or side to sleep.
  • Firm around arms, loose around hips — tight swaddling around the legs increases the risk of hip dysplasia. Choose a hip-healthy swaddle like the ergoPouch Cocoon or Love to Dream Swaddle Up, both IHDI-certified.
  • Never above the shoulders — the swaddle must sit below the neck and cannot ride up to cover your baby's face.
  • Stop when rolling starts — the moment your baby shows any sign of rolling (usually 3-4 months), stop swaddling immediately. Read our complete guide on when to stop swaddling.
  • Dress for the temperature — check the TOG rating of your swaddle. For Australian summers use 0.2 TOG, for winters use 2.5 TOG. Overheating increases SIDS risk. A good rule: one more layer than you'd wear to sleep yourself.

How to Swaddle with Arms Up vs Arms Down

Not every baby likes the same swaddle style. Here's how to choose the right technique for your little one.

The Classic Arms-Down Wrap

Best for babies with a strong startle reflex. Arms are wrapped straight down at their sides. Use a traditional muslin wrap or a velcro swaddle like the SwaddleMe Original (AU$20-23) for the quickest arms-down swaddle.

The Arms-Up Swaddle

Some babies sleep better with their hands near their face — the same position they held in the womb. The Love to Dream Swaddle Up (AU$45-50) is designed specifically for this with patented wings that let babies self-soothe by touching their face.

Common Swaddling Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too tight around the hips — the #1 mistake. Your baby's legs should bend up and out freely. Tight wrapping increases hip dysplasia risk. Always choose IHDI-certified swaddles when possible.
  • Too loose around the arms — if your baby can wriggle an arm free, the swaddle isn't doing its job. For muslin wraps, pull each corner firmly before tucking.
  • Wrong TOG for the season — a 2.5 TOG swaddle in a 30°C Australian summer bedroom is dangerous. Check the temperature and match the TOG.
  • Swaddling past the rolling stage — Red Nose Australia is clear: stop immediately when rolling starts. A swaddled baby who rolls onto their tummy cannot roll back.
  • Using a loose blanket instead of a designed swaddle — loose bedding in the cot is a SIDS risk. Always use a purpose-made swaddle or a tightly wrapped muslin that can't come loose. If you can slide more than two fingers between the wrap and your baby's chest, it's too loose.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I swaddle my baby safely?

Always place your baby on their back to sleep. Keep the swaddle firm around the arms but loose around the hips so their legs can bend naturally. Stop swaddling as soon as your baby shows signs of rolling. Never swaddle above the shoulders — the fabric must sit below the neck and cannot cover their face.

At what age should I start swaddling my newborn?

You can start swaddling from birth. Most Australian hospitals will show you how before you go home. Swaddling is most effective in the first 3-4 months.

Should I swaddle with arms up or down?

It depends on your baby. Some prefer arms-up (natural womb position), others settle better with arms wrapped down. Try both — the Love to Dream Swaddle Up is designed for arms-up, while the SwaddleMe Original is arms-down.

How tight should a swaddle be?

Firm around the arms to control the startle reflex, loose around the hips and legs. Red Nose Australia says your baby's legs should bend up and out freely. Too tight around the hips increases hip dysplasia risk.

Can I swaddle my baby in an Australian summer?

Yes — use a lightweight 0.2 TOG swaddle or a single-layer muslin wrap. Avoid fleece or heavy blankets. In very hot conditions (30°C+), a short-sleeve bodysuit under a light swaddle is usually enough.
