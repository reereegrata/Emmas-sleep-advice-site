Baby Sleep Schedule by Age, Your Complete 0-12 Month Guide for Australian Parents

By Emma · Updated July 2026 · 9 min read
Home › Guides › Baby Sleep Schedule by Age

Figuring out when your baby should sleep — and for how long — is one of the hardest parts of the first year. Every few months, everything changes. The routine that worked last week suddenly stops, and you're back to square one.

This guide covers baby sleep schedules from newborn to 12 months, with hourly sample routines, wake windows, nap transitions, and regression coverage for every stage. All recommendations follow Red Nose Australia, Tresillian, and Karitane guidelines — written by one Aussie mum who's been through every stage.

How Baby Sleep Changes in the First Year

Your baby's sleep isn't just about being tired — it's driven by biology, and that biology changes dramatically over the first 12 months. Understanding what's happening behind the scenes makes it much easier to work with your baby's natural rhythms rather than fighting against them.

In the first three months, your baby has no circadian rhythm. They don't produce melatonin — the sleep hormone — in a day-night pattern. This is why newborns sleep in short bursts around the clock and can't tell day from night. According to the Raising Children Network, the circadian rhythm starts developing around 3–4 months and is largely mature by 6 months.

Sleep cycles also change. A newborn's sleep cycle is roughly 50 minutes — half the length of an adult's 90-minute cycle. This means newborns surface into light sleep (or wakefulness) twice as often. By 4–6 months, sleep cycles mature to the adult-like 90-minute pattern, which is why the 4-month regression hits — your baby's brain is literally rewiring how it sleeps.

Because of these biological shifts, sleep schedules need to change every few months. A routine that works beautifully at 8 weeks will fall apart at 16 weeks — and that's normal, not a sign you've done something wrong.

Newborn Sleep Schedule: 0–3 Months

The newborn stage is about survival. In the first six weeks, forget about schedules. Your baby needs to feed frequently, sleep when they're tired, and wake when they're hungry. This is normal, not a problem to fix.

Here's what typical newborn sleep looks like:

  • Total sleep: 14–17 hours in a 24-hour period
  • Wake windows, 0–4 weeks: 30–60 minutes (including feeding time)
  • Wake windows, 4–8 weeks: 45–75 minutes
  • Wake windows, 8–12 weeks: 60–90 minutes
  • Night wakes: Every 2–4 hours for feeding — completely normal

These wake window ranges are based on Tresillian's guidance for Australian families. The most important thing: watch your baby, not the clock. If your baby is yawning, rubbing their eyes, staring into space, or grizzling — that's your cue to start settling, regardless of what the clock says.

Sample Schedule: 8–12 Weeks

By 8–12 weeks, many babies start settling into a rough pattern. Here's what a typical day might look like:

Night feeds: expect 2–3 wake-ups for feeding. Keep night feeds quiet, low-light, and all-business — no playtime at 2 AM.

Day/Night Confusion

If your newborn treats 3 AM like party time, you're not alone. Day/night confusion happens because their circadian rhythm hasn't kicked in yet. The fix is simple and effective:

  • Expose your baby to morning sunlight — open the curtains, go outside for a few minutes after the first feed
  • Keep daytime feeds in a bright, normal-noise environment
  • Keep night feeds dark, quiet, and boring — no talking, no eye contact, no nappy change unless it's dirty
  • Start a simple bedtime routine from 6–8 weeks: bath, feed, swaddle, cuddle, bed

Introducing Feed-Play-Sleep

The feed-play-sleep cycle is a simple pattern to prevent feeding-to-sleep associations. After your baby wakes: feed them first, then have a short play period (nappy-free time, tummy time, a song), then watch for tired signs and settle for sleep. In the newborn stage this might be feed-play-sleep-play-feed — don't stress if it's messy. The goal is just to avoid feeding your baby to sleep every single time.

Red Nose safe sleep for newborns: Always place your baby on their back to sleep, in their own cot or bassinet with a firm, flat mattress. No loose bedding, pillows, bumpers, or soft toys. Room share (baby in your room, in their own cot) for at least the first 6–12 months. Swaddle safely — firm around the chest but loose around the hips so baby can bend their legs. Stop swaddling as soon as baby shows signs of rolling. For the complete guidelines, see our Safe Sleep & SIDS Prevention Guide.

Baby Sleep Schedule: 4–6 Months

Around 4 months, everything changes — and not because you've done anything wrong. This is when the 4-month sleep regression hits, and it's one of the biggest sleep disruptors in the first year.

The 4-Month Sleep Regression

The 4-month regression isn't a phase — it's a permanent neurological change. Your baby's sleep cycles are maturing from newborn (50-minute) cycles to adult-like (90-minute) cycles with distinct light and deep sleep stages. Between cycles, your baby wakes more fully — and if they relied on being rocked or fed to sleep, they'll need that same help every 90 minutes all night long.

This regression typically lasts 2–6 weeks, according to Karitane. It won't go away on its own — your baby needs to learn how to link sleep cycles. For a deeper dive, see our complete 4-month sleep regression guide.

Wake Windows & Sleep Needs

  • Wake windows: 1.5–2.5 hours (gradually extend over this period)
  • Total sleep: 12–15 hours per 24 hours
  • Naps: 3–4 naps per day, transitioning from 4 to 3
  • Night sleep: 10–12 hours with 0–2 feeds (some babies can night-wean with GP approval)

Sample Schedule: 5–6 Months (on 3 Naps)

6:30 AMWake, breast/bottle feed
7:30 AMSolids breakfast — small amount (rice cereal, puréed pear)
8:45 AMNap 1 (1–1.5 hours)
10:15 AMWake, breast/bottle feed
12:00 PMSolids lunch, then play
1:00 PMNap 2 (1.5–2 hours)
3:00 PMWake, breast/bottle feed
4:45 PMNap 3 — short catnap (30–45 min)
5:30 PMWake, solids dinner
6:15 PMBath, quiet play, final breast/bottle feed
7:00 PMBedtime routine — story, cuddle, into cot awake
Wake windows: ~2 hours between naps, ~2.25 hours before bed. If the third nap is consistently refused, your baby may be ready for the 3-to-2 nap transition (see 7–9 months section).

The 4-to-3 Nap Transition

Most babies drop the fourth nap between 4–5 months. Signs your baby is ready: the fourth nap becomes a battle, bedtime is pushing past 8:30 PM, or the fourth nap is so short it barely counts (under 20 minutes). To transition: stretch wake windows by 10–15 minutes across the day so the remaining three naps absorb the sleep pressure. Expect an earlier bedtime (6:00–6:30 PM) for a week or two while your baby adjusts.

Starting Solids and Sleep

Starting solids around 4–6 months can affect sleep in both directions — some babies sleep better with a fuller tummy, others have discomfort from new foods. A few tips:

  • Introduce new foods at lunch, not dinner — gives you the day to spot reactions
  • Avoid offering solids right before a nap — give at least 45 minutes for digestion
  • Don't expect solids to magically fix sleep — hunger is rarely the primary disruptor at this age

Tresillian and Karitane both recommend establishing a consistent routine during this period, with predictable feed and sleep times. Your baby is old enough to start learning patterns.

Baby Sleep Schedule: 7–9 Months

If 4–6 months is the hardest sleep period, 7–9 months is the busiest. Your baby is likely crawling, sitting independently, maybe pulling to stand — and their brain is firing on all cylinders. This is when the 8-month regression (which can hit any time from 7–9 months) shows up.

The 8-Month Regression

The 8-month regression is driven by three things hitting at once: separation anxiety (your baby now understands you exist when you leave the room and they don't like it), motor milestones (crawling, pulling to stand, cruising — they'd rather practise than sleep), and teething (those top teeth are brutal).

Unlike the 4-month regression, this one is behavioural rather than neurological. Your baby isn't rewiring their sleep — they're just too busy and too aware to settle easily. This regression typically lasts 2–4 weeks and can be managed with consistency and patience.

Wake Windows & Sleep Needs

  • Wake windows: 2.25–3.5 hours
  • Total sleep: 12–14 hours per 24 hours
  • Naps: 2–3 naps, transitioning from 3 to 2
  • Night feeds: 0–2 (most babies can manage 0–1 night feeds by 9 months with GP approval)

Sample Schedule: 8 Months (on 2 Naps)

☀️ Sample Schedule — 8 Months
6:30 AMWake, breast/bottle feed
7:30 AMSolids breakfast (porridge, toast fingers, fruit)
9:30 AMNap 1 (1–1.5 hours)
11:00 AMWake, breast/bottle feed
12:00 PMSolids lunch, then play
2:00 PMNap 2 (1.5–2 hours)
3:45 PMWake, breast/bottle feed, afternoon snack
5:30 PMSolids dinner

7:00 PMBedtime — story, cuddle, into cot awake
Wake windows: ~3 hours before first nap, ~3 hours between naps, ~3.25 hours before bed. If your baby is still on 3 naps, the third nap should be a short 20–30 minute bridging nap with a slightly later bedtime.

The 3-to-2 Nap Transition

Most babies drop from 3 naps to 2 between 6–9 months. Signs your baby is ready:

  • The third nap (late afternoon catnap) is consistently refused or takes 20+ minutes of settling
  • Bedtime is pushing past 8:00 PM because the third nap is too late
  • Your baby wakes early (before 6:00 AM) despite good daytime naps — the third nap may be stealing night sleep pressure
  • Your baby can comfortably manage a 2.5–3 hour wake window before bed

To transition: gradually push the morning nap later (aim for 9:30 AM) and the afternoon nap later (aim for 2:00 PM). Drop the third nap and bring bedtime forward to 6:00–6:30 PM temporarily. The transition usually takes 1–2 weeks to settle.

Teething and Sleep

Teething disrupts sleep, but it shouldn't destroy it for weeks on end. If your baby is waking hourly all night, it's unlikely to be just teeth. Practical tips beyond "give Panadol":

  • Offer a cold washcloth or chilled teething ring before the bedtime feed
  • If using pain relief, give it 30 minutes before bedtime so it kicks in during settling
  • Keep your sleep routine consistent — don't introduce new sleep associations just because teeth are coming through
  • Teething pain peaks for 3–5 days per tooth, not weeks — if disruption lasts longer, look for other causes

Separation Anxiety at Bedtime

Separation anxiety peaks around 8–9 months and can make bedtime suddenly difficult. Your baby cries the moment you leave, stands up in the cot calling for you, and resettles the second you return — only to cry again when you leave.

Try responsive settling — stay in the room with a hand on your baby, gradually reduce your involvement over several nights. For step-by-step techniques, see our Responsive Settling Guide.

Baby Sleep Schedule: 10–12 Months

You've almost made it through the first year. At 10–12 months, your baby is likely cruising along furniture, maybe taking first steps, babbling with intent, and developing a real little personality. Sleep-wise, this age brings the 11-month regression and the early signs of the 2-to-1 nap transition.

The 11-Month Regression

Like the 8-month regression, this is driven by development: walking or cruising, a language explosion (understanding words, maybe saying a few), and another wave of separation anxiety. You might find your baby standing in the cot at 2 AM, "practising" new skills instead of sleeping.

This regression typically lasts 1–3 weeks. Don't introduce new habits — if you bring your baby into your bed during the regression, expect that pattern to stick.

Wake Windows & Sleep Needs

  • Wake windows: 3–4 hours
  • Total sleep: 12–14 hours per 24 hours
  • Naps: 2 naps per day (most babies this age are firmly on 2 naps)
  • Night feeds: 0–1 (most 10–12 month olds don't need night feeds nutritionally)

Sample Schedule: 10–11 Months

Wake windows: ~3.5 hours before first nap, ~3.25 hours between naps, ~3.25 hours before bed. If your baby consistently fights the morning nap for 2+ weeks, they may be showing early 2-to-1 nap readiness (see below).

Is My Baby Ready for One Nap?

This is the most common question from parents of 10–12 month olds. For most babies, 11 months is too early to drop to one nap. The 2-to-1 nap transition typically happens between 13–18 months. What looks like nap refusal at 11 months is more likely the regression or a schedule tweak needed.

Genuine signs of readiness for one nap (not just regression):

  • Consistent morning nap refusal for 2+ weeks — not just a few days or during a leap
  • Morning nap extends to 2+ hours and afternoon nap is completely refused
  • Bedtime is consistently past 8:30 PM even with capped naps
  • Your baby can manage a 4.5–5 hour wake window in the morning

If your 11-month-old fights naps, try capping the morning nap at 45–60 minutes to preserve the afternoon nap before dropping naps entirely. Most babies at this age still need two sleeps.

When They Practise in the Cot

If your baby wakes at night and immediately pulls to stand, cruises along cot rails, or babbles — they're practising, not distressed. During the day, give lots of floor time for walking and cruising so they're less driven to do it at night. Lower the cot mattress to the lowest setting once your baby pulls to stand. If they're standing and calling out, go in, lie them back down, offer a brief pat, and leave. The more you engage, the more you reinforce the 2 AM play session.

Thinking about transitioning to a toddler bed? Don't rush it. Most children sleep better in a cot until at least 2.5–3 years. The cot-to-bed transition is about safety (a child climbing out is at risk of falling), not age. If your toddler isn't climbing out, the cot is still the safest, most sleep-conducive option. Toddler Sleep: 12–36 Months (Brief Overview)

Toddler sleep deserves its own guide — and we've written one. Here's a quick overview of what to expect from 12–36 months.

Regression Timeline

Sleep regressions don't stop at 12 months. The major ones:

  • 12-month regression: Walking, first words, separation anxiety — lasts 1–3 weeks
  • 18-month regression: The big one — language explosion, independence, may include night terrors. Can last 2–6 weeks
  • 2-year regression: FOMO (fear of missing out), testing boundaries, imagination developing — may involve bedtime battles rather than night waking
  • 3-year sleep shift: Dropping the last nap, transitioning to quiet time — this is more of a permanent schedule change than a regression

Nap Transitions

  • 2-to-1 nap: 13–18 months — this is the hardest nap transition. Stick with it.
  • Dropping the last nap: 2.5–4 years — move to "quiet time" instead of cold-turkey nap removal

Sleep Needs & The Toddler Stall

Toddlers need 11–14 hours of total sleep per 24 hours. The "toddler stall" — those elaborate bedtime delay tactics ("one more story," "I need water," "my toe hurts") — peaks between 2–3 years. The fix: a firm but warm bedtime routine, a clear boundary ("two stories, then lights out"), and consistency. Giving in one night and holding firm the next teaches your toddler that persistence pays off.

Safe Sleep at Every Age (Red Nose Australia)

Safe sleep isn't just for newborns. Every sleep stage has its own safety considerations. Here's what Red Nose Australia recommends at each age:

Newborn (0–3 Months)

  • Back to sleep — always place your baby on their back, for every sleep
  • Firm, flat mattress in a cot or bassinet that meets Australian safety standards (AS/NZS 2172 for portable cots, AS/NZS 2195 for folding cots)
  • No loose bedding, pillows, bumpers, or soft toys in the cot
  • Room share for at least the first 6–12 months — baby in their own cot in your room
  • Safe swaddling — firm across the chest, loose around the hips. Stop swaddling at the first sign of rolling

4–6 Months

  • Once your baby can roll from back to tummy, stop swaddling immediately. Transition to an arms-out baby sleeping bag
  • If your baby rolls onto their tummy during sleep, you don't need to roll them back — as long as they got there themselves and the cot is free of hazards
  • Continue room sharing

7–12 Months

  • Lower the cot mattress to the lowest setting once your baby can sit or pull to stand — prevents climbing falls
  • No cot bumpers — they're a suffocation and strangulation risk at any age
  • Remove mobiles and anything hanging above the cot once your baby can reach them
  • Room sharing can continue to 12 months; after 12 months, the risk of SIDS is very low and room sharing is no longer considered necessary

Toddler (12–36 Months)

  • Cot safety: If your toddler can climb out of the cot (legs over the rail), transition to a toddler bed or floor mattress — this is about preventing falls
  • Bed safety: Use a bed rail initially. Keep the sleep space free of cords, strings, and heavy bedding
  • Red Nose guidelines apply until 12 months; after that, a clear, uncluttered sleep space is always safest

Frequently Asked Questions

What are wake windows and how do I use them?

Wake windows are the time your baby can comfortably stay awake between sleeps — starting at 30–90 minutes for newborns and stretching to 3–4 hours by 10–12 months. Watch for tired signs (eye rubbing, yawning, grizzling) as the window ends, and start settling before overtiredness hits. Tresillian recommends using wake windows as a rough guide while following your baby's cues first.

How long should newborn wake windows be?

Thirty to 60 minutes in the first 4–6 weeks, extending to 60–90 minutes by 8–12 weeks. In the early weeks, your baby may only manage 30–45 minutes — including feeding time — before needing sleep again. Watch for tired signs (jerky movements, staring, grizzling) rather than watching the clock.

Is the 4-month sleep regression real and how long does it last?

Yes — it's linked to your baby's sleep cycles maturing from 50-minute newborn cycles to 90-minute adult-like cycles. Babies wake more fully between cycles and struggle to link them without help. It typically lasts 2–6 weeks. Stay consistent with your routine and avoid introducing new sleep associations during this phase.

Is 11 months too soon to drop to one nap?

For most babies, yes. The 2-to-1 transition usually happens at 13–18 months. Nap refusal at 11 months is more likely the 11-month regression than true one-nap readiness. Try capping the morning nap at 45–60 minutes before dropping naps entirely.

What is the 5-3-3 rule for babies?

A nighttime feeding guideline: wait 5 hours after bedtime before the first night feed, then 3 hours, then 3 hours. This helps distinguish hunger from habit waking. It's generally used for babies 4+ months with GP or child health nurse approval. Always respond to genuine hunger during growth spurts.

When should I move my baby from 3 naps to 2?

Most babies are ready between 6–9 months. Signs: consistently fighting the third nap, bedtime pushing past 8:00 PM, early waking despite good naps, or managing 2.5–3 hour wake windows. Drop the third nap, push the first two naps later, and expect a 6:00–6:30 PM bedtime during the 1–2 week transition.

How do I know if my baby's sleep schedule is working?

If your baby is happy, rested, and feeding well, the schedule is working — even if it doesn't match the textbook.
