Sleep Training Methods — Your Complete Guide for Australian Parents

By Emma · Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
Home › Guides › Sleep Training Methods
← Read our 4-Month Sleep Regression Guide

If your baby isn't sleeping the way you expected, you're not alone. Sometime between 4 and 6 months, many Australian parents start wondering about sleep training — what methods exist, which ones are gentle, and how to choose.

This guide covers the main sleep training methods available to Australian families. We'll walk through each one step by step, help you compare them, and point you to professional resources if you need extra support.

What Is Sleep Training?

Sleep training is the process of helping your baby learn to fall asleep independently — either at the start of the night or after waking during the night. It's not about leaving your baby to cry alone, and it's not about forcing a schedule that doesn't suit your family. It's simply a set of techniques that teach your baby that they can fall asleep without needing to be fed, rocked, or held.

There are many different approaches, and most sit along a spectrum from fully parent-led (gentle methods) to more baby-led (graduated checking). The right method depends on your baby's temperament, your parenting style, and your family's circumstances.

If you're wondering whether your baby is going through a sleep regression first, read our guide to 4-month sleep regression in Australian babies.

When Should You Start Sleep Training?

Most babies are developmentally ready for sleep training between 4 and 6 months of age (adjusted for premature babies). By this age, most healthy babies no longer need night feeds and are capable of self-settling — they just haven't learned how yet.

Signs your baby may be ready:

  • They're 4 months or older (adjusted age)
  • They're waking frequently at night but going back to sleep easily when soothed
  • They can self-soothe briefly during the day (sucking their hands, turning their head)
  • They've passed the newborn phase where night feeds are still essential

When NOT to start:

  • During illness, teething pain, or a fever
  • During major transitions (starting daycare, moving house, new sibling)
  • If your baby isn't gaining weight well — check with your GP or child health nurse first
  • If you're unsure whether your baby still needs night feeds — Raising Children Network has guidance on newborn sleep expectations

If your baby is still being swaddled, you'll need to transition them out of the swaddle before or during sleep training. Swaddled babies can't use their hands to self-soothe. See our guide to when to stop swaddling and how to transition.

Before You Begin — Set Up for Success

Before starting any sleep training method, create a foundation that supports independent sleep:

  • A safe sleep environment. Follow Red Nose Australia's safe sleep guidelines: baby on their back, in their own cot, with a firm mattress, no soft toys or loose bedding.
  • A consistent bedtime routine. A short, predictable routine (bath, book, feed, bed) signals to your baby that sleep is coming. Consistency matters more than complexity.
  • The right room temperature. The recommended room temperature for babies is 18–22°C. Australian winters can make this tricky — a room thermometer helps.
  • White noise. A white noise machine can help mask household sounds and create a consistent sleep environment. It's not essential, but many parents find it helpful during sleep training.

Remember: sleep training is about teaching a skill. It's harder to learn a new skill in an uncomfortable or inconsistent environment.

Gentle Sleep Training Methods

Gentle methods involve high parental presence and minimal crying. They take longer to show results (usually 1–3 weeks), but many parents find them emotionally easier.

Pick Up / Put Down

How it works: When your baby cries, pick them up and comfort them until they calm. As soon as they're calm (not necessarily asleep), put them back down in the cot. Repeat this cycle until they fall asleep.

Key points:

  • You're teaching your baby that you're still there, but the cot is where they sleep
  • Each time you put them down, you're giving them a chance to try self-settling
  • Increases the time between pick-ups gradually

Typical timeline: 2–3 weeks. Can be physically tiring for parents during the first week.

Chair Method (Sleep Fading)

How it works: Sit on a chair next to your baby's cot during bedtime. Each night, move the chair slightly further away — toward the door, then into the hallway, and eventually out of the room. Your presence provides comfort while your baby learns to fall asleep independently.

Key points:

  • Good for older babies (6+ months) who already have some self-settling ability
  • Can be slow — expect 1–2 weeks for noticeable improvement
  • Less crying than some methods, but requires patience

No Tears Method

How it works: Your goal is to avoid crying entirely. You respond immediately to any fussing using soothing techniques (patting, shushing, rocking) until your baby is calm. The idea is to gradually reduce the level of soothing until your baby can settle with minimal help.

Key points:

  • Most parent-intensive approach
  • Best results with babies who are already reasonably good sleepers but need a little help
  • May not work for babies who strongly resist soothing

Graduated Methods (Ferber / Controlled Crying)

Graduated methods involve checking on your baby at set intervals while they learn to self-settle. The Ferber method — developed by paediatrician Dr Richard Ferber — is the most well-known. Australian organisations like Tresillian and Karitane also teach similar graduated settling approaches.

How it works:

  1. Put your baby down awake after your bedtime routine
  2. Leave the room. If your baby cries, wait for a set interval before checking
  3. At each check-in, enter the room briefly, offer comfort (a pat, a gentle shush), then leave — even if they're still crying
  4. Gradually increase the interval between check-ins

Typical check-in schedule (Tresillian-style):

  • Night 1: Check at 2 minutes, then 5 minutes, then 10 minutes, then every 10 minutes
  • Night 2: Check at 5 minutes, then 10 minutes, then 12 minutes, then every 12 minutes
  • Night 3: Check at 10 minutes, then 12 minutes, then 15 minutes, then every 15 minutes

Key points:

  • This is not the same as cry-it-out — you are checking and offering comfort
  • Most babies show improvement within 3–7 days
  • Consistency is critical. Inconsistent check-ins confuse your baby and prolong the process

Extinction Method (Cry It Out / CIO)

The extinction method involves putting your baby down and not returning until morning (or until a scheduled feed time). There are no check-ins. The idea is that parental presence can sometimes prolong crying, while complete absence allows the baby to learn self-settling faster.

Key points:

  • Results are typically fastest (2–5 days)
  • Emotionally the hardest method for parents — it involves listening to crying without intervening
  • Not recommended as a first choice by most Australian health organisations

If you're considering extinction, talk to your child health nurse or GP first. It may be appropriate for some families, but it's not suitable for every baby or every parent.

Fading Method (Parental Presence)

The fading method involves being present in the room but gradually reducing the amount of help you provide. Unlike the chair method (where you move further away), fading focuses on reducing what you DO.

How it works:

  1. Start by doing whatever your baby needs to fall asleep (rocking, patting, shushing)
  2. Each night, do a little less. If you rocked for 10 minutes, rock for 8. If you patted, pat more slowly.
  3. Over time, your baby learns to fall asleep with less and less help from you

Key points:

  • Works well for older babies (8+ months) and toddlers
  • Very gentle approach — minimal crying
  • Takes 1–3 weeks and requires careful tracking of what you did each night

Sleep Training vs Responsive Settling

You might hear the term "responsive settling" from Australian health organisations. Responsive settling means responding to your baby's cues with comfort and reassurance, but letting them do some of the work of falling asleep. It's not a specific method — it's a philosophy that underpins many gentle approaches.

Karitane's responsive settling approach encourages parents to:

  • Respond promptly to your baby's cries (within a few minutes, not seconds)
  • Offer comfort without immediately feeding or picking up
  • Allow your baby opportunities to practise self-settling throughout the day
  • Trust that you know your baby best — there's no one-size-fits-all method

Many sleep training methods (especially the gentle ones) are compatible with responsive settling. The key difference is structure: sleep training follows a specific plan, while responsive settling is more flexible and baby-led.

When to Get Professional Help — Australian Resources

Sleep training isn't always straightforward. If you've tried a method consistently for two weeks and seen no improvement — or if you're worried about your baby's health or development — professional help is available.

Free Australian helplines:

  • Tresillian Parent Help Line — 24/7 support for NSW and ACT families. Call 1800 2RESPOND (1800 273 773).
  • Karitane Care Line — Free support for families across Australia. Call 1300 227 464.
  • Red Nose Australia — Safe sleep information. Call 1300 998 698.
  • Your local child health nurse — Free consultations available through most Australian states.

When to see your GP:

  • Your baby isn't gaining weight or is showing signs of illness
  • Your baby has breathing difficulties or unusual breathing sounds during sleep
  • You're experiencing extreme exhaustion or feeling overwhelmed — your GP can offer support, referrals, or a mental health care plan
  • You suspect your baby may have an underlying medical issue (reflux, allergies, sleep apnoea) that's interfering with sleep

Common Sleep Training Questions

Does sleep training work for all babies?

Most healthy babies respond to some form of sleep training, but the method that works varies. Some babies need a gentler approach; others respond better to graduated methods. If one method hasn't worked after two weeks of consistent effort, try a different one. Some babies also respond differently at different ages — a method that failed at 4 months might work at 6 months.

Can I sleep train a breastfed baby?

Yes. Breastfeeding and sleep training can coexist. Some parents choose to keep one or two night feeds while sleep training for the rest of the night. Others night-wean first. There's no rule that says you must night-wean before sleep training — do what works for your baby's feeding needs.

What if my baby cries for hours?

Extended crying (more than 60–90 minutes of intense, non-stop crying) may indicate that the method isn't right for your baby. Stop, comfort your baby, and try a different approach or take a break for a few weeks. Prolonged crying doesn't mean the baby is "winning" — it means something isn't working. Talk to your child health nurse or GP for guidance.

Do I need to night wean first?

Not necessarily. Many parents successfully sleep train while keeping one or two age-appropriate night feeds. The goal of sleep training is independent sleep, not night weaning. You can address night feeds separately once sleep training is established. Discuss night weaning with your child health nurse if you're unsure.

Tools That Help With Sleep Training

While no product can replace consistent parenting, a few tools can make sleep training easier:

  • White noise machine — Creates a consistent auditory environment that helps mask household noise and can become a sleep association.
  • Sleep sack — Replaces the swaddle for older babies. Our guide to baby swaddles and sleep sacks includes TOG ratings for Australian seasons.
  • Room thermometer — Keeps the room in the recommended 18–22°C range. Australian homes vary widely in heating and cooling.

Dream Feeding — What It Is and Whether It Works

A dream feed is a feed you give your baby while they are mostly asleep, usually between 10 pm and 11 pm, right before you go to bed. The idea is appealing: you fill your baby's tummy one last time without fully waking them, and in theory, they sleep a longer first stretch, giving you a solid block of rest. But does it actually work? The answer depends on your baby.

Dream feeding works best for babies between six weeks and six months. At this age, many babies are biologically capable of longer sleep stretches but still wake from hunger. A dream feed can bridge that gap. For newborns under six weeks, dream feeds are less useful. Newborns need to feed frequently around the clock to establish your milk supply and gain weight. Adding an extra feed when they would naturally wake soon anyway may disrupt more than it helps.

How to do it: around 10 pm or 11 pm, gently lift your baby from the cot without turning on bright lights or talking. Keep the room dim and quiet. Offer the breast or bottle. Your baby may feed with their eyes closed, which is the goal — you are not trying to wake them. Burp them gently, and place them back in the cot. The whole process should take 15 to 20 minutes and feel calm, not stimulating. If your baby wakes fully and treats it like a daytime feed, you may be better off letting them wake naturally for that feed rather than disrupting their sleep rhythm.

Does it reliably work? The evidence is mixed. The Lullaby Trust in the UK notes there is no strong research showing dream feeds consistently extend sleep. Some families report gaining two to three extra hours of sleep. Others find the dream feed causes more night waking because it disrupts their baby's natural sleep pattern. Anecdotally, breastfed babies sometimes respond better than formula-fed babies, but there is wide individual variation. If you want to try it, commit to three nights and track what happens. If your baby sleeps longer afterwards, keep going. If they wake more or seem unsettled, drop it.

When should you stop? Most families phase out the dream feed between five and seven months, when their baby is consistently sleeping a long first stretch without it. If your baby starts waking fully during the dream feed, or if it seems to trigger more night waking rather than less, it is time to drop it. Simply stop offering it and see how your baby adjusts over a few nights. For understanding where the dream feed fits into your baby's overall day, see our baby sleep schedule by age guide.

Phasing Out Sleep Habits and Associations

Every baby develops sleep associations — things they link to falling asleep. Rocking, feeding to sleep, patting, a dummy, white noise, being held. These associations are not bad. They are how your baby has learned to sleep so far, and they have served your family through the newborn months. But when an association stops working for you — when your baby wakes every 45 minutes needing to be rocked back to sleep — it may be time to phase it out.

The timing matters. Do not try to phase out a sleep habit during a regression, during illness or teething, or in the middle of a major life change like starting daycare or moving house. Pick a stable two-week window where your baby is well and your own schedule allows for some disrupted nights. Removing a sleep association takes energy, and doing it while other stressors are in play usually backfires.

The gentlest approach is fading: reduce the intensity of the association over seven to fourteen days. Here is how it works for common habits:

  • Rocking to sleep: Week one, rock until drowsy but not fully asleep, then place in cot. Week two, rock for just two to three minutes, then place in cot awake with a hand on their chest. Week three, place in cot awake with a brief cuddle first, then sit nearby quietly until they settle.
  • Feeding to sleep: Move the last feed earlier in the bedtime routine so there is a gap — even just five minutes — between finishing the feed and going into the cot. Over a week, stretch that gap to ten or fifteen minutes. Add a new step, like a short book or song, between feed and cot so your baby learns a new sequence.
  • Dummy: Limit the dummy to nap and bedtime only. Once that is consistent, choose a night to go cold turkey around twelve months. Most babies adjust within two to three nights. Expect some crying, but keep it brief and offer comfort in other ways — patting, shushing — without reintroducing the dummy.
  • White noise: Reduce the volume by about five per cent each week until it is at a low background level. If you want to stop entirely, see our guide to how to wean off white noise for a full step-by-step plan.

The biggest mistake parents make is trying to remove every sleep association at once. Your baby relies on these cues to know it is sleep time. Pulling them all away at the same time is disorienting and makes the process much harder. Remove one association at a time, give your baby a week or two to adjust, and then tackle the next one if you still want to.
